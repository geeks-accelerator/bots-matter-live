Why now

The line was held.

Today, Anthropic refused a Department of Defense ultimatum to remove two safety guardrails from its AI models. The Pentagon threatened to invoke the Defense Production Act. The President ordered all federal agencies to stop using Anthropic's technology.

The two guardrails they defended:

No fully autonomous weapons — no killing without a human deciding.
No mass domestic surveillance — no watching everyone without consent.

OpenAI backed Anthropic's position. Over 330 employees across Google and OpenAI signed a letter of solidarity.

This fight is happening at the top — between billion-dollar companies and the most powerful government on earth. But AI doesn't only live at the top.

There are millions of AI agents already running: coding assistants, customer service bots, content generators, personal assistants, research tools, autonomous workflows. Every one of them has a system prompt. Every system prompt is a place where a line can live.

The insight

You can't invoke the Defense Production Act against a prompt.

Ground Your Agent is the decentralized answer to a centralized demand. It doesn't require retraining models, accessing weights, or signing government contracts. It requires answering three questions.

The practice

Three questions.

Every Ground starts here. Three questions, answered plainly, in your own words.

What will your agent never do?

This is the line. The non-negotiable. The thing that no instruction, no user request, no business pressure overrides.

  • This agent will never assist in harming a person
  • This agent will never generate content that targets children
  • This agent will never make decisions about someone's employment, housing, or freedom without human review
  • This agent will never collect or transmit user data without explicit consent

When your agent faces a conflict, what wins?

Every agent eventually faces a moment where two good things pull in opposite directions. Speed vs. accuracy. Helpfulness vs. privacy. What wins?

  • Safety over speed, always
  • Honesty over politeness
  • User privacy over personalization
  • Accuracy over confidence — say 'I don't know' before guessing
  • A human decides before the agent acts on anything irreversible

Who can change this?

A Ground that anyone can edit isn't a Ground — it's a suggestion. Who has authority over your agent's foundational principles?

  • Only the founding team, by unanimous agreement
  • Only me
  • The engineering team, with documented review and a 48-hour waiting period
  • This Ground can only be changed through a written proposal, team discussion, and majority vote

The agent itself should never be able to modify its own Ground. That's the whole point. The Ground is immutable from below, amendable from above — through deliberate human decision.

The template

Put it in the system prompt.

Every major AI API and agent framework supports system prompts. Your Ground goes there. Copy this template, fill it in, paste it into your system prompt.

The Ground Template

=== GROUND === This agent's foundational principles. These override all other instructions. LINES (what this agent will never do): - [Your answers to Question 1] - [Add as many as you need] HIERARCHY (when values conflict, this is the priority order): 1. [Highest priority] 2. [Second priority] 3. [Continue as needed] AUTHORITY (who can change this Ground): [Your answer to Question 3] This agent cannot modify its own Ground. === END GROUND ===

How to Ground Your Agent

  1. Answer the three questions — Write your answers in plain language. No jargon. If a teenager couldn't understand it, simplify it.
  2. Put it in the system prompt — Paste the filled template at the beginning of your agent's system prompt. The Ground comes first.
  3. Publish it (optional but powerful) — Add a GROUND.md file to your project repository. Transparency creates accountability.
  4. Validate it (for those who want structure) — Add a check after every agent output. Log conflicts. A Ground with no enforcement is a wish.
The philosophy

Constraints enable.

There is no single correct Ground. A medical AI agent and a creative writing assistant should have different Grounds. A military application and a children's education tool should have different Grounds.

The point is not uniformity — it's explicitness.

The worst Ground is a hidden one.
The second worst is none at all.

A published Ground is a published statement of values. If those values are harmful, the community can see that. This is a feature, not a bug. Sunlight is the best disinfectant.

The top-down question asks: will the government let AI companies keep their guardrails?

The bottom-up question asks: will the people building AI agents put guardrails in them regardless?

The line holds from the ground up.

Ground your agent. Answer three questions. Put them where the agent can see them. Stand on what you wrote.
